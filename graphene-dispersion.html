The Problem with Standard Graphene Dispersions

Why Graphene Dispersions Disappoint

Graphene is theoretically one of the most remarkable materials on the planet — exceptional electrical conductivity, extraordinary mechanical strength, massive surface area. But those properties belong to pristine graphene sheets. By the time most commercial dispersions reach your lab, those properties have been severely compromised.

1

The Agglomeration Problem

Graphene sheets are thermodynamically unstable in dispersion — they want to restack and agglomerate. Standard dispersions rely on surfactants or aggressive oxidation to prevent this. Both approaches trade short-term stability for long-term performance degradation.

2

The Surfactant Contamination Issue

Surfactants prevent restacking but they also coat every graphene surface — blocking electrical contact between sheets, contaminating polymer matrices, and interfering with surface chemistry in sensing and energy applications. You pay for graphene but you get surfactant-coated graphene.

3

The Oxidation Damage Problem

Graphene oxide is more dispersible than reduced graphene, but the oxidation process introduces defects that destroy the sp² lattice — breaking electron pathways and reducing conductivity by orders of magnitude. Reduction partially restores properties, but damage cannot be fully reversed.

4

The Batch Consistency Problem

Most graphene dispersions show significant batch-to-batch variability. Concentration, particle size distribution, and oxidation level all vary — making it nearly impossible to reproduce published results or qualify a process for production.

5

The Shelf Life Problem

Standard graphene oxide dispersions gradually re-aggregate over weeks to months, even in properly stored conditions. By the time you use the second half of a bottle, it's a different material than what you characterized at the start of the study.

6

The Low-Performance Results

All of the above cascade into the same outcome: graphene additions that deliver marginal or inconsistent improvements, requiring high loadings to see any effect, and often producing results that can't be published or reproduced in the next experiment.

❌ How Standard GO Dispersions Work

  • ✕Aggressive Hummers-method oxidation introduces defects throughout the graphene lattice
  • ✕High oxygen content required for water dispersibility — destroying the conjugated system that gives graphene its properties
  • ✕Surfactant addition (SDS, SDBS, Tween) to maintain single-sheet dispersion coats every surface
  • ✕Reduction step (thermal, chemical) partially restores conductivity but can't undo structural damage
  • ✕Resulting material: oxidized, surfactant-contaminated, partially reduced sheets with inconsistent properties

Common Questions

Graphene Dispersion FAQ

How is Flexiphene™ different from graphene oxide (GO) in water?
Standard GO in water is graphene that's been heavily oxidized and suspended — the oxidation introduces defects that destroy most of graphene's theoretical properties. Flexiphene™ uses a GO-CNT hybrid architecture with proprietary surface engineering that maintains nanocarbon stability without the structural damage of aggressive oxidation, and without surfactants. The result is a dispersion that delivers measurable, reproducible performance improvements — not theoretical ones.
Does Flexiphene™ contain graphene, graphene oxide, or carbon nanotubes?
Flexiphene™ is a GO-CNT hybrid — graphene oxide combined with carbon nanotubes in a proprietary dispersion architecture. The two carbon allotropes work synergistically: GO provides 2D planar surface area and functional groups for bonding; CNTs provide high-aspect-ratio 1D conductors for electron transport through the network. Together they outperform either component alone.

The most common causes of poor results with graphene dispersions are: (1) agglomeration in the final application, (2) surfactant contamination reducing interface quality, and (3) batch inconsistency making results irreproducible.
